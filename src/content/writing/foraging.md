In this modern day and age, the average convenience store might be just five minutes away. It's easy to rely on fast food in our busy working lives. So why would someone want to take up foraging? What do you have to gain?

## What Is Foraging?

Most people picture foraging as someone wandering around, picking at plants, and stuffing them into bags. But foraging is an art form — the practice of collecting food from the wild.

It's a fantastic, expansive opportunity to add highly nutritious foods to your diet, ones bursting with flavour and minerals. And it brings you closer to the nature around you. In fact, it's one of the most rewarding hobbies out there.

## Who Can Forage?

Anyone and everyone keen to get a little dirt under their nails. Those who want to escape the bustling city life — so many of us are trapped inside a concrete jungle. It also makes a wonderful family activity, taking you far from the addictive screens, placing you outdoors, and helping you save on the grocery bill.

## Where Can You Forage?

Anywhere there are plants around. That said, you'll want to avoid common parks (where pets like to do their business) and roadsides (often tarnished by petrol fumes). Be aware, too, of land that may have been exposed to pesticides.

Your own backyard is the safest place to start, and you can expand outward as you gather more information about the sites you'd like to explore.

There's one foraging law worth knowing: you'll need the landowner's permission before digging up any roots.

## What Are the Benefits?

For one, foraging gets you outdoors in the fresh air — a real bonus in a society that spends most of its working day inside. It slows down time and gives you room to breathe and focus.

The foods themselves are packed with nutrition. For example, just fifty grams of kale gives you everything you need of vitamins A and C — and many humble weeds share the same benefits as cultivated greens.

You'll also experience tastes unlike anything before. We're all limited to a handful of domesticated fruits and vegetables, but there's an entire world of fresh ingredients out there. Imagine coming across blackberries or wild cherries on your walk home — the fresh, pure taste, the smell, the joy of discovery.

## Plants in Your Backyard You Didn't Know Were Edible

Around 400,000 plant species on earth are edible — eye-opening, when most of us stick to the same fruits, vegetables, and grains. In fact, most people only eat around 0.1% of the world's edible plants. Prepared properly, even the common acorn is edible.

For a sense of how accessible this can be, take a wander around your backyard and look out for:

- **Dandelion** — a tough plant that's nearly impossible to get rid of, but makes for an easy harvest. Known for its medicinal uses, or chop the (well-washed) leaves into a salad.
- **Wood sorrel** — not related to the sorrel family, but sharing a lovely tangy flavour with succulent, soft leaves.
- **Violets** — easy to identify by their striking purple colour. All parts of the viola are edible, including the delicately flavoured flowers, and they pop up in gardens all over the world.

Start small, start safe, and let the world become your pantry.
