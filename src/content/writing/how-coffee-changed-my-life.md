Would you believe that coffee pulled me out of a toxic environment? This simmering brew of legal addiction changed my life — but not in the way you'd think.

To start it all, I was an apprentice at a hair salon. Three years into my training, I kept getting delayed on my certification because I had to switch jobs and came face to face with a global pandemic. As soon as lockdown lifted, I returned to work — and slowly but surely, my life inside that environment started to spiral, and I went down with it. There's no doubt the beauty industry is filled to the brim with toxic bosses and harrowing workplaces, with a few rare gems mixed in between.

For context: a salon works best with an operator for each stylist — someone to wash the client's hair, apply the foils, and so on. The apprentice takes a third role, helping both the stylist and the operator while learning on the job.

When I came back, I was the sole apprentice *and* operator in a salon of four stylists. I was expected to learn, watch, and keep the whole area clean and tidy all at once. They claimed to "help" me clean, but their half-hearted help often got me into trouble. I worked overtime constantly, lunch breaks weren't a thing, and I started living for my days off. Even at home, I carried a permanent sense of dread about the next shift.

Every piece of feedback turned more and more negative, until I was dealing with daily passive aggression from my boss and one of the stylists. My mental health was taking a serious toll, and I was ready to quit the hair industry for good.

Many of my peers told me to stick around and "see it through" — unaware, or unwilling, to fully understand my situation. Some of that was on me; I didn't share much of what I was going through.

One day I tried to confront one of the stylists about how they were treating me, only to have the whole thing turned around on me. I hid in the bathroom stall, in tears, and called my aunt.

She drove over that evening with cake, wine, and a very good piece of advice. She asked whether I thought I was capable of doing hair on my own.

That opened a door — a possibility I couldn't stop turning over. The stress had stolen my appetite and left me waking up nauseous. It got to the point where I was tempted to drive into oncoming traffic just to avoid one more day at work. I had a panic attack one evening.

And despite all of it, I still decided to stick it out — until my grandfather passed away. He was close and dear to me. I took the Thursday off to help with the funeral arrangements, and I wanted Friday too, to keep helping and to be with my family.

I texted my boss to organise it. She simply said I was expected in on Friday, with absolutely no remorse.

She'd crossed a line. I knew then I was going to quit. But I had a debt to pay off, which meant I couldn't leave without something else to keep me afloat. Still, just *deciding* to leave changed everything in my head. It meant I no longer cared what they told me, or how hard they tried to wear me down. This was a temporary job now, and the moment I found something new, I'd take it.

I knew someone who ran a small barista business at the farmer's market and was looking for weekend help. I messaged him on the Monday, asking to apply.

I remember the day he replied — immediately open to the idea. I was ecstatic. And despite being scolded that day for being "on my phone" instead of watching a hair wash I'd done a thousand times (and earned generous tips for), nothing she said could break my joy. I arranged a few early-morning hours at the stall before my salon shifts.

I'd found my escape — as a coffee maker.

That Wednesday, we spring-cleaned the whole salon, and I told my boss I needed to talk. I sat down, handed over my resignation, and said, *"I'm giving my week's notice."* The thing is, I didn't even have to. I'd signed no contract — even though they were legally obligated to provide one. Nothing actually bound me there. But out of respect, I planned to leave the following Wednesday and start my part-time job.

On Thursday I attended my grandfather's funeral. By then, so much damage had been done that I couldn't even grieve properly — I was too consumed by everything at work. I knew his loss would hit me later.

Friday rolled around, and for once the passive aggression stopped. They barely spoke to me, and I was happy just to be the operator. I wasn't being tortured anymore. It was peaceful. For the first time in my life, I was glad to be ignored.

Saturday came, and I got up at 3:30 to reach the market by 4:15. It was early, with a fresh chill in the air. I filled the portafilter with grounds and had the time of my life. Then I left for the icy atmosphere of the salon — but I was happy. It was going to be my last weekend there.

The salon day was hectic, but I felt content. My boss wanted to speak to me, and little did I know she was going to try to talk me out of leaving. She started by saying I knew my work. I knew exactly where it was heading.

I stood my ground. She got upset, and some of her last words were: *"I wouldn't want anyone who knew nothing to work for me anyway."* I grabbed my things and walked out. Later she texted me to drop off the work items on Tuesday.

And that was it. The end of a terrifying workplace, and the start of a brand-new one.

My time as a barista was filled with good laughs and fond memories. It was completely new, but my hard work at the salon had prepared me for the hard work at the stall. My love for coffee grew. I finally learned the difference between a latte, a cappuccino, and an Americano. It wouldn't be far-fetched to say that job handed me the axe to cut the rope binding me to something that was hurting me.

Which brings me to this: even when you feel trapped, you might just be missing the window of opportunity. The door in front of you might be shut — but you hold the key to the one behind you.

Life has had its fair share of twists and turns. But the day I got that message about the barista job, and the day I walked out of that salon, was the day my life changed forever for the better. It was only then that I truly realised how dangerous a place I'd been in mentally — and how much a work environment can make or break a person.

So to all the women in the beauty industry: I salute you for lasting. But I also plead with you to work with kindness and empathy in mind. For such a woman-dominated space, I never imagined I'd be treated that way. Of the five salons I worked in, only two were memorable for the right reasons. The other three left me with scars.
