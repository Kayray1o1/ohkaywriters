*Being the best means you have to be right — all the time, right?*

No.

This is a dangerous philosophy that runs rampant among entrepreneurs, managers, and so many others chasing success in today's corporate world. I once bought into it myself — a desperate urge to always be right that cost me friendships and opportunities. Over time, I came to see the importance of genuine human connection.

But the mindset is still wildly popular in business, where high-stress environments kick our need to perform perfectly into gear. The trouble is that it eventually comes back to bite you — this time from people who hold the same philosophy and don't much care who they cross to reach their destination.

## Understanding the Binary Thinking Pattern

Always needing to be right is part of the binary thinking trap, where everything is seen as right or wrong, black or white. This mindset pushes us to trust our own opinions above all else, and we miss chances to build real connections or solve problems more efficiently. Realising there are many ways to reach the same destination is the first step to breaking free.

I think of it as a swinging pendulum: we're far more likely to trust our own thoughts over anyone else's, never realising what we lose by refusing to meet people in the middle. I've always believed life is a balance — and meeting people in the middle means letting go of black-and-white thinking.

## The Psychological Mechanism

Our attachment to our ideas is powerful. When someone challenges an idea we cherish, it can feel like a personal attack. That knee-jerk defensiveness often does more harm than good — especially when the criticism was meant to help us improve. Sometimes biting our tongue isn't about submission; it's about creating space for better solutions and deeper connections.

There's another reality I can't dismiss: sometimes people's opinions really are an attack on your character rather than your idea. That's a pitfall of the working world too big to solve here, so I've saved it for another article. But once you determine that the feedback is genuinely about the *idea*, it's best not to be too attached. See your ideas as mouldable, bendable, and approachable — every piece of work can be refined into the best version of itself.

## Letting Go of the Need to Always Be Right

Your ideas will be:

- Disagreed with
- Challenged
- Explored
- Discarded and reshaped
- Played around with

Ideas aren't meant to be permanent — they're meant to be explored, edited, and altered. You already do this to your own ideas constantly. Now it's time to let others work on them the same way. Separate yourself from your ideas, and understand that your opinions matter but can still change.

Remember, too, that our ideas are built on our own point of view and experience. When we present them to people with different backgrounds, they can see consequences and flaws we simply can't. Being open to that is a gift. Poking holes in an idea is what lets us patch it into something that actually works.

The more open you are to this, the stronger the connections you'll build with like-minded people — and the more opportunities you'll get to present your ideas to others, perhaps in more influential rooms than your own.

## Moving Forward

The choice between being right and fostering connection isn't just about style — it's about creating an environment where diverse perspectives can flourish. Accepting that our thoughts aren't infallible paves the way for richer conversations, deeper collaboration, and more innovative outcomes.

I live by the belief that networking and genuine connection trump the need to be right. Sometimes, letting go is the best way to grow.
