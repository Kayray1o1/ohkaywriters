Perfectionism is considered both a flaw and a tool — yet I've seen it do far more damage in the pursuit of getting something so "correct" that even more mistakes get made along the way.

I'm not a perfectionist myself, but I'm surrounded by so many that it's a topic I feel comfortable writing about. Whether it's floods of feedback on how things could improve, or people questioning why I set something up a certain way — when, for them, it didn't make sense — I've seen the pattern up close.

Much of that feedback doesn't change anything significant. And yet it leaves me frustrated, sometimes throwing my hands in the air. It made me wonder: how does perfectionism truly affect a career? When you're in the working world, surrounded by a team, surely it can't always be a good thing?

So I did a deep dive. What I found was fascinating — and mildly alarming.

## Fixed Mindset and Perfectionism — How Are They Connected?

*"I'm just a perfectionist."*

I've heard people say this all the time. And yet I've rarely, if ever, heard someone say, *"I'm trying to stop being a perfectionist."*

So it's not far-fetched to suggest that being a perfectionist often goes hand in hand with a fixed mindset. The thoughts, actions, and habits built on perfectionism feel set in stone, because *"that's just the way you are."*

> People with a fixed mindset consider abilities and skills as "fixed" traits. Because of this, they usually don't focus on change or development, and they don't want to work beyond the scope of their role. Thus, they tend to back down and avoid a challenge whenever it arises. — *Shortlister*

Put simply, perfectionists rarely aim to change this about themselves, and few people challenge them on it. But it can grow into a serious problem — because perfectionism has real drawbacks. As long as someone believes it can't (and shouldn't) be changed, that belief becomes the very first thing sabotaging their career.

## Five Ways Perfectionism Can Sabotage Your Career

It's good to have an eye for detail. It's good to care about safety. Some roles genuinely require a degree of perfectionism to function. But the kind I'm talking about is a step beyond the essentials — into the areas where you could survive perfectly well without everything being 100% in place.

> Perfectionism is characterized by striving for flawlessness and setting excessively high standards for performance, accompanied by tendencies for overly critical evaluations of one's behavior. — *Flett & Hewitt, 2002*

### 1. Procrastination

Given the impossibly high standards perfectionists set, I'm not surprised procrastination is so common among them. The crippling pressure to get things right is often enough to stop people from starting at all.

And procrastination in a career is simply detrimental — pushing things to the last minute, missing deadlines, increasing anxiety, and sometimes producing worse work than if you'd started early. The irony is brutal: chasing "perfect" eats the very time you needed, and as the window shrinks, so does the quality. A vicious, deadly cycle.

### 2. Burnout Through Over-Preparation

I've faced many burnouts in my life, and I have no doubt that perfectionists who pile pressure on themselves face even more — often at the times they can least afford it.

> Workaholism and perfectionism have increasingly been identified as significant obstacles to effective lifelong learning and skills development, ultimately undermining long-term career adaptability and organizational resilience. — *MDPI*

Other factors feed this too — job insecurity being a big one. These pressures fuel the perfectionistic nature until it becomes both a habit and a justification: if I don't deliver something perfect, I risk losing my job or facing displeased employers. As the pressure stacks, burnout eventually follows — a perfectionist's worst nightmare, since it can leave you unable to work at your best, or at all.

*The lesson isn't to stop caring about your work. It's to notice when "perfect" has quietly become the thing standing between you and doing it.*
